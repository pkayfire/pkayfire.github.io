I've recently begun reading regularly. I found it dull initially, almost painful, but I guess that goes to show how inundated I've been with all the sensationalized journalism[^1] going around these days. 

One of the books I've enjoyed reading is *[Contagious: Why Things Catch On]* by Jonah Berger. In it, Berger coins the concept of **social currency**, the reason why we're so inclined to discuss our attitudes and experiences:

> "It's more than just vanity; we're actually wired to find it pleasurable. Harvard neuroscientists Jason Mitchell and Diana Tamir found that disclosing information about the self in intrinsically rewarding... They found that sharing personal opinions activated the same brain circuits that respond to rewards like food and money."

What, however, validates social currency, especially in social media? Surely there exists a method to validate that blog post you wrote, photo you uploaded, or status you shared. A quick glance through one's Facebook or Twitter feed reveals the answer-- likes, comments, shares. Although imperfect, it serves as a quantifiable **feedback mechanism**. The model is the following: the higher the *engagement* on your post, the higher the *validity* of your social currency, the higher your *frequency* of future posts.

Some people treat social currency validation as **zero-sum**. If they engage with your post, they're potentially undermining their reputation and content. The average case is that both parties usually don't come out winners. 

Validation should not be considered zero-sum. It's positive sum, where validating someone's social currency makes them more likely to attempt to validate yours in the future-- thereby establishing a mutually beneficial transaction. You might occasionally expose content that your audience doesn't enjoy, but the expected return over time should be considered **positive**.

In the words of Dale Carnegie's famous *[How to Win Friends and Influence People]*:

>"Become genuinely interested in other people."

When the next opportunity arises, I know what I'll be *commenting*.

Hey ____, <br>
**Thanks for sharing.**

*tl;dr validating social currency is not zero-sum*
